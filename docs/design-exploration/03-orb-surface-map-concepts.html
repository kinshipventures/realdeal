Orb Surfaces
Five Alternatives to the Radial Gradient
The glow halo and health ring are locked — these are about what the orb surface itself looks like. Each shown with the glow halo effect applied.

A. Flat + Rim Light

Pure flat color with a thin bright edge on the upper-left — like a backlit object. No interior gradient at all. The rim catches imaginary light. Minimal but dimensional.

Clean and modern. The rim is the only "effect" — everything else is flat. Confident. Most Trolley-PDF-faithful.

B. Matte + Noise Grain

Flat color with a subtle noise texture overlay. Feels organic, tactile — like you could reach out and touch the surface. Think album art or risograph printing.

Gives orbs a physical, printed quality. The grain adds warmth without adding a "shiny" effect. Pairs well with the editorial serif direction.

C. Two-Tone Color Shift

A diagonal gradient from the base color to a slightly shifted hue — not lighter/darker, but color-shifted. Red shifts toward orange. Purple shifts toward blue. Each orb has internal color motion.

More vibrant than flat, less cliche than specular. Each orb has its own internal energy. Closest to "Spotify Wrapped" for the orb surface itself.

D. Dead Flat (let the halo work)

Zero surface treatment. Pure solid color, nothing else. All the visual interest comes from the glow halo and health ring — the orb itself is just a circle of color. Maximum confidence.

E. Inset Depth (concave impression)

Inset box-shadow creates the feeling of a concave surface — like the color is pressed INTO the circle rather than painted on. Subtle top highlight + bottom darkening via shadow only, no gradients. Unique physicality.

Map Concepts
Four Conceptually Different Canvases
Not color variations — different spatial metaphors for how a network feels. Each changes what "exploring your relationships" means.

A. Orbital System

Concentric orbit rings emanate from the hub. Priority pods orbit closer, lower priority further out. The hub has gravity — it pulls what matters closer. Like a solar system for relationships.
The network has gravity. What's close matters most. Drill-in could zoom to an orbit ring, expanding it into category orbs. Outer pods literally recede in importance.

B. Constellation Map

Orbs as stars on a dark canvas. Dotted constellation lines connect them. A subtle coordinate grid underneath. The network is a sky you navigate — your personal star chart of relationships.
The network is a sky. Cross-connections between pods show relationships between categories (talent ↔ service providers). Drill-in could zoom into a constellation, revealing individual stars (contacts).

C. Radar Pulse

Radar sweep emanating from the hub. Orbs appear at different distances based on recency of contact — recently touched pods pulse closer to center, neglected ones drift outward. The map IS the equity score, visualized spatially.
The map IS the data. Distance = neglect. Pods drift outward when you stop touching base. The sweep animation could reveal "pings" when interactions happen. Most functional of the concepts.

D. Topographic Landscape

Contour lines flow between orbs like a terrain map. Each pod is an "island" — the contours show relationship density between them. Busier paths have tighter contours. Your network has geography.
The network has terrain. Each pod is a peak with contours showing its "influence radius." Overlapping contours show where pods connect. Light canvas, editorial feel. Most unique of the four — nobody maps relationships as geography.
